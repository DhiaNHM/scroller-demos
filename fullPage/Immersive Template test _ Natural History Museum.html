Getting ready to dive

At the surface, beaked whales breathe out stale air through their blowholes and breathe in fresh air to take in the oxygen they need to survive a long, deep dive.

Save muscle oxygen

Beaked whales store oxygen in their muscles, stowing energy for long dives even after lung collapse. Their streamlined shape helps to save muscle oxygen and makes movement efficient.

The full lungs of beaked whales make them float. They need to use a lot of energy to overcome this buoyancy in order to swim down.

By a depth of 200 metres the air inside their lungs has been squashed so much by increasing pressure that they have collapsed.

The deep ocean is too dark to spot prey. Beaked whales make echolocation 'clicks' to sense their preyby passing air through noise makers in their heads called phonic lips.

After diving so deep, beaked whales must choose between continuing to feed or resurfacing for air. If they use all their muscle oxygen, recovery will take much longer.
